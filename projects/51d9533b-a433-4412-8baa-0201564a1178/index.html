Unmatched Bandwidth

Copper cables max out around 10 Gbps over 100 meters. Single-mode fiber handles 100+ Gbps across distances measured in kilometers.

Immunity to Interference

Fiber optic signals are immune to electromagnetic interference, providing reliable performance in electrically noisy environments.

Lower Latency

Light signals through glass travel faster than electrical signals through copper, reducing end-to-end network latency.

GPON Architecture

How GPON Works

OLT - Central Office

Optical Line Terminal at the service provider hub connects to the fiber distribution network. Manages bandwidth allocation and protocol translation.

Passive Splitter

No power required. Divides one fiber input into 32 or 64 outputs. Typical split ratios are 1:32 or 1:64.

ONU/ONT - Customer

Optical Network Unit (business) or Optical Network Terminal (residential) at the subscriber premises.

Traffic Flow

Downstream

2.488 Gbps broadcast to all ONUs

Upstream

1.244 Gbps shared via TDMA

GPON uses asymmetric speeds — downstream is 2x faster than upstream

P2P Architecture

Point-to-Point Networks

Dedicated Fiber Paths

Each endpoint has its own dedicated fiber running directly back to the central switch. No sharing of bandwidth.

Full Bandwidth Available

100% of link capacity is available to each subscriber at all times. No competition for shared resources.

Native Ethernet Protocol

Simpler protocol stack — pure Ethernet from end to end. No special PON encapsulation needed.

Infrastructure Requirements

100 Subscribers 100 Fiber Pairs

Evaluation

Decision Framework

Use these criteria to guide your technology selection

Subscriber Density

High-density areas (apartments, offices) favor GPON. Low-density rural deployments may favor P2P economics.

Bandwidth Requirements

Users needing >1 Gbps symmetric should consider P2P. GPON's 2.5 Gbps shared downstream works for most applications.

Scalability Needs

GPON offers easy upgrades via wavelength stacking (XGS-PON, NG-PON2). P2P requires additional fiber runs for growth.

Vendor Ecosystem

GPON has more managed service providers. P2P offers more vendor flexibility with standard SFP+ optics.

Key Question: How many subscribers per fiber feeder?

Summary

Key Takeaways

GPON: Cost-Efficient Shared Access

Best for high-density deployments with multiple subscribers sharing fiber infrastructure. Lower initial cost, passive components, proven carrier-grade reliability.

P2P: Maximum Performance

Best for dedicated high-bandwidth connections where every bit matters. Lower latency, full bandwidth, simpler troubleshooting — at higher infrastructure cost.

The Choice Depends on Your Context

No universal winner exists. Evaluate subscriber density, bandwidth needs, budget constraints, and future scalability when making your decision.
